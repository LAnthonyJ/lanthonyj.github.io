Customer Churn Survival Analysis with PySpark & IBM Telco Data

April 28, 2026 • Big Data Software & Applications • 12 min read

1. Introduction to Survival Analysis

Survival analysis is a statistical methodology for analyzing time-to-event data. Unlike standard regression models, survival analysis gracefully handles censoring — observations where the event has not yet occurred by the end of the study period. In business contexts, this translates directly to customer churn prediction: we want to know not just if a customer will churn, but when.

Key concepts:

  • Survival time T: Time from study entry to event (here: tenure in months)
  • Event indicator δ: 1 = churned, 0 = still active (censored)
  • Survival function S(t) = P(T > t): Probability a customer stays past month t
  • Hazard function h(t): Instantaneous churn rate at month t, given survival to t
Why does censoring matter? If we simply drop customers who haven't churned yet, we lose valuable information — knowing a customer survived at least 72 months without churning tells us something important. Censoring lets us use all available data without bias.

2. Dataset: IBM Telco Customer Churn

The IBM Telco Customer Churn dataset contains 7,043 customers from a telecommunications company, with 21 features covering demographics, account information, and subscribed services. For this analysis, we apply a Silver Table filter: keeping only month-to-month contract customers with internet service, yielding 3,351 records. The churn rate in this filtered cohort is 46.4% (censoring rate 53.6%).

3. Setting Up PySpark & Data Loading

from pyspark.sql import SparkSession
from pyspark.sql import functions as F
from pyspark.ml.feature import VectorAssembler, StringIndexer, OneHotEncoder
from pyspark.ml import Pipeline
from pyspark.ml.regression import AFTSurvivalRegression
from lifelines.fitters.coxph_fitter import CoxPHFitter

spark = SparkSession.builder \
    .appName("SurvivalAnalysis_Telco") \
    .config("spark.driver.memory", "2g") \
    .getOrCreate()

# Load raw data
df_raw = spark.read.format("csv") \
    .option("header", "true") \
    .load("Telco-Customer-Churn.csv")

# Silver table: filter + cast + encode
df_silver = df_raw \
    .withColumn("ChurnBinary",
        F.when(F.col("Churn") == "Yes", 1.0).otherwise(0.0)) \
    .filter(F.col("Contract") == "Month-to-month") \
    .filter(F.col("InternetService") != "No") \
    .na.fill({"TotalCharges": 0.0})
# Result: 3,351 rows, churn rate 46.4%

4. Kaplan-Meier Survival Estimation

The Kaplan-Meier (KM) estimator is the standard non-parametric method for estimating survival functions:

S(t) = ∏ti ≤ t (1 − di / ni)

Where di is the number of churn events at month ti, and ni is the number of customers still at risk just before ti.

Stratified KM: DSL vs Fiber Optic

Splitting by internet service type reveals a clear pattern: DSL customers consistently have higher survival probabilities than Fiber optic customers across all time points. This may reflect price sensitivity (Fiber optic costs more) or different service quality expectations.

Key Insight Fiber optic churn risk is notably higher. Telecom companies should consider targeted retention offers for Fiber optic subscribers, especially during the first 6 months when churn is most intense.

5. Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox Proportional Hazards (Cox PH) model is a semi-parametric approach that estimates hazard ratios without assuming a specific baseline distribution:

h(t | x) = h0(t) · exp(xTβ)

A Hazard Ratio (HR) < 1 means the factor is protective (reduces churn risk); HR > 1 means it increases risk. We fit the model using lifelines.CoxPHFitter, as recommended by the Databricks Solution Accelerator tutorial.

All four covariates have HR < 1, meaning they are protective factors:

  • OnlineBackup (HR=0.46): Customers with online backup are 54% less likely to churn — the strongest protector
  • TechSupport (HR=0.53): Having tech support reduces churn risk by 47%
  • Dependents (HR=0.72): Customers with dependents are 28% less likely to churn
  • DSL Internet (HR=0.80): DSL users churn 20% less than Fiber optic users

Concordance Index = 0.64, indicating the model has moderate discriminative power (significantly better than random at 0.50).

6. AFT Survival Regression (PySpark MLlib)

The Accelerated Failure Time (AFT) model parameterizes the relationship between covariates and survival time:

log(T) = xTβ + σε   (ε ~ Weibull)

A positive coefficient means the variable extends survival time (protective). A negative coefficient means it shortens survival time (risk factor).

Feature Engineering Pipeline

We apply StringIndexer + OneHotEncoder to 13 categorical features (InternetService, Contract, PaymentMethod, OnlineSecurity, OnlineBackup, DeviceProtection, TechSupport, StreamingTV, StreamingMovies, gender, Partner, Dependents, PaperlessBilling) and combine them with 3 numeric features (SeniorCitizen, MonthlyCharges, TotalCharges) via VectorAssembler, producing a 43-dimensional feature vector. 

# PySpark ML Pipeline for feature engineering
stages = []
for c in cat_cols:
    indexer = StringIndexer(inputCol=c, outputCol=c+"_idx", handleInvalid="keep")
    encoder = OneHotEncoder(inputCol=c+"_idx", outputCol=c+"_vec", handleInvalid="keep")
    stages += [indexer, encoder]

assembler = VectorAssembler(inputCols=encoded_cols + num_cols, outputCol="features")

pipeline = Pipeline(stages=stages)
model_df = pipeline.fit(df_silver).transform(df_silver)

# Train AFT model

7. Model Evaluation

Note on Evaluation Standard RMSE/R² metrics behave poorly with survival data because they cannot account for censoring. When a censored customer has actual tenure=6 but the model predicts 400 months (which may be reasonable!), RMSE explodes. The gold standard metric for survival models is the C-index (Concordance Index), which measures the model's ability to correctly rank customers by their churn risk, unaffected by censoring.

8. Individual Survival Prediction

The trained AFT model can predict personalized survival curves for any customer profile. We compare three risk profiles:

  • Low Risk (feature mean × 1.2): high online security, tech support, online backup; DSL internet; credit card payment → median survival significantly longer
  • High Risk (feature mean × 0.8): streaming services, electronic check payment; Fiber optic; no ancillary services → survival probability drops below 50% by ~20 months

This enables the telecom company to target high-risk customers with retention incentives before they churn — a concrete business application of survival analysis.

9. Conclusions

  1. Kaplan-Meier curves reveal that ~87% of month-to-month internet customers eventually churn (S(71)=0.1266). DSL customers consistently out-survive Fiber optic customers.
  2. Cox PH model identifies four protective factors: OnlineBackup (HR=0.46), TechSupport (HR=0.53), Dependents (HR=0.72), and DSL service (HR=0.80). Concordance Index = 0.64.
  3. AFT regression reveals StreamingMovies and electronic check payment as the strongest risk factors, while OnlineSecurity, TechSupport, and SeniorCitizen status act as protective factors.
  4. Telecom companies should prioritize ancillary service bundling (OnlineBackup + TechSupport) and early engagement for Fiber optic customers paying via electronic check.
